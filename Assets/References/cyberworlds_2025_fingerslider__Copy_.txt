FingerSlider: A Single-Handed Multi-Attribute
Gesture Interface for Intuitive VR Control
Bofei Huang, Mikiya Kusunoki, Haoran Xie
Japan Advanced Institute of Science and Technology, Ishikawa, Japan
Email: s2520016@jaist.ac.jp, s2420019@jaist.ac.jp, xie@jaist.ac.jp

Abstract—The control interfaces of virtual reality (VR) control interfaces have a fundamental trade-off between control
naturalness and precision. This paper proposes FingerSlider, a
novel single-handed interaction system that integrates optical
hand tracking with adaptive parameter mapping to control
virtual objects through finger movements. Each finger is mapped
to a specific control parameter (e.g., the index finger for the
X-axis, the middle finger for the Y-axis), transforming finger
movements into virtual sliders. FingerSlider enables simultaneous
adjustment of multiple object attributes (position, scale, rotation)
while maintaining high control precision and reducing cognitive
load. The proposed approach dynamically maps thumb-to-finger
sliding distances to control parameters through vector projection
and normalization, achieving real-time performance with low
computational complexity. Comparison experiments demonstrate
that FingerSlider significantly outperformed conventional methods, reducing task completion time by 43% in single-parameter
tasks (24.5s vs. 43.0s). While the initial four-finger prototype revealed limitations in multi-finger coordination (SUS score: 58.75),
the optimized two-finger system achieved high usability (SUS
score: 82.81), representing a 40.95% improvement. These findings
suggest potential applications of FingerSlider in various VR tasks,
including 3D object manipulation and content browsing.

I. I NTRODUCTION
Virtual Reality (VR) is increasingly adopted in diverse
fields, including medical simulation and industrial design [1],
[2]. As VR evolves, gesture-based interactions have gained
popularity, offering more natural and intuitive control by
aligning with human motor habits and reducing reliance on
physical controllers [3], [4]. However, a challenging issue in
VR gesture control is balancing naturalness with precision required for complex tasks [3]. Current interfaces often struggle
with precision and efficiency, particularly for multi-attribute
adjustments or fine-grained manipulations, limiting their utility
in professional, accuracy-critical scenarios.
Existing VR gesture interfaces predominantly use direct or
indirect manipulation paradigms [5]–[7]. Direct manipulation,
while intuitive, often lacks precision for detailed adjustments
or distant interactions [8], [9] and can induce physical fatigue, commonly referred to as “gorilla arm syndrome” [10].
Conversely, indirect methods, such as ray casting or virtual
sliders, can improve precision and reduce strain. Still, they
may compromise immersion and increase cognitive load due to
the mental mapping required between hand actions and virtual
controls. This highlights a core VR interface design challenge:

II. R ELATED W ORK
A. Direct and Indirect Manipulation
VR interaction techniques are categorized as direct or
indirect, reflecting distinct approaches to spatial interaction
and user cognition. Direct manipulation mimics physical operations with immediate visual feedback and minimal abstraction [12], prioritizing visualization, rapid feedback, and
reversibility [13]. This aligns with real-world habits, enhancing intuitiveness [14]. However, it suffers from precision
limitations for small objects, “gorilla arm syndrome” during
prolonged use [10], and restricted workspace due to limited
hand movement range [15]. Indirect manipulation employs
intermediary techniques, such as ray casting, virtual sliders,
or body-attached interfaces [7], enabling precise control and
interaction with distant objects [16]. Features like snapping
enhance precise alignment [17], valuable in engineering education [18], while reducing physical fatigue [10]. Though
potentially disrupting immersion and increasing cognitive load,
indirect methods can foster higher-order thinking in complex
tasks [17]. We adopted this approach for FingerSlider due
to its superior precision and control for fine adjustments and
beyond-reach interactions, despite steeper learning curves.
B. Slider Interfaces
Slider interfaces in VR/AR enable continuous parameter
adjustments and can be categorised into three main types:
traditional virtual sliders, body-attached interfaces, and onobject interfaces. Traditional sliders range from 2D projections
to 3D spatial objects, striking a balance between familiarity,
precision, and immersion. Dachselt and Hübner [19] proposed
3D sliders that enhance spatial interaction but lack precision in tactile feedback. Body-attached interfaces like palm
menus [20] or arm-based markers [21] reduce task interruption
but may increase cognitive load. On-object interfaces integrate
controls directly onto virtual objects. Examples include wrist
angle-based slider control for content reading [22], which reduces fatigue but needs accuracy verification; GesPlayer [23],
using finger movements for video control with unverified
VR effectiveness; and UbiEdge [24], which places sliders on
physical object edges for AR interaction but faces environmental limitations (e.g., lying down, absent edges). Universal
applicability and precision remain challenging. Sliders show

potential across VR applications including visual design [25],
animation [26], and immersive reading [27]. FingerSlider
addresses these challenges by combining traditional slider
precision with natural gesture interaction, minimizing visual
interference and fatigue for complex VR tasks.
III. P ROPOSED M ETHOD
We propose FingerSlider, a novel gesture-based interface
that transforms finger movements into virtual sliders for
intuitive parameter control in VR. As shown in Figure 2,
the system supports two primary operation modes: (a) with
the hand in a side profile relative to the tracker for optimal
finger discrimination and (b) with the palm facing the tracker
as an alternative posture. Both modes utilize thumb-againstfinger sliding motions, blending natural gesture interaction
with traditional slider precision.
A. Research Question and Hypotheses
Despite advancements in VR manipulation, traditional indirect controls can be limited in their precision adjustment
for multi-attribute settings and may cause fatigue, especially
when working with large virtual objects or performing finetuning in multiple dimensions. Existing gesture interfaces also
often underutilize finger dexterity. This leads to our research
question:
Can integrating indirect multi-attribute control with
finger-based sliding gestures enhance efficiency and user
experience in VR environments compared to traditional
gesture-based controls? Accordingly, we formulate the following hypotheses:
H1 The proposed interface performs more efficiently than
traditional gesture-based VR controls.
H2 The proposed interface offers a more intuitive and
preferred user experience than traditional gesture-based
controls.
H3 The proposed interface improves spatial awareness and
parameter adjustment accuracy in VR tasks.
H4 The proposed interface allows for a faster learning curve
and reduced operational time compared to traditional
gesture-based controls.

B. System Overview
FingerSlider leverages optical hand-tracking to convert
thumb-to-finger sliding movements into a multi-attribute control mechanism. The system uses optical hand tracking for
multi-attribute control by detecting fine finger movements.
Users can modify parameters through a thumb-sliding gesture
along the lateral surface of their fingers. Specifically, users
may place their thumb tip against the side of a finger (starting
at the fingertip) and slide it toward the middle joint to
adjust the corresponding parameter. The fingertip serves as
the slider’s start point and the middle phalanx as its endpoint
(Figure 1). During operation, the fingers remain relatively
stationary while the thumb performs the sliding motion along
the finger’s surface, creating a stable reference frame for
precise control. The speed of thumb movement can affect the
parameter adjustment velocity. Tapping any part of the fingers
can skip animations.

C. Implementation Details

D. Finger-Attribute Mapping Design

The FingerSlider prototype uses an Oculus Quest 2 headset,
a Leap Motion controller (v5.13.2) for hand tracking (integrated via a custom 3D-printed mount), and a PC (Intel i711800H, RTX 3070 GPU). Components connect via USB 3.0
Type C for low-latency tracking. Figure 4b shows the Leap
Motion centrally fixed on the Oculus Quest 2 for cohesive
visual representation and intuitive capture.
Gesture recognition was implemented in Unity (version
2023.1.18f1) using the Ultraleap SDK (version 6.13.0). The
system initializes in a “no hand” state, switching to a “hand”
state upon detection. Users control applications by sliding their
right thumb tip component against the sides of their fingers.
For slider design (Figure 4a), we used Leap Motion’s ghost
hand model. Based on average adult finger lengths (e.g., index
finger 7.29 cm, middle 7.90 cm) [28], we set slider lengths
at 7 cm for the index, middle, and ring fingers, and 5.5 cm
for the little finger. Sliders are 8 mm in height and width for
easy triggering. Interactive buttons within the sliders share this
height and width, with a length of one-tenth the total slider
length. A 1 cm diameter green sphere on the thumb tip serves
as the interactor, highlighting buttons upon capture and moving
them along the slider bar with thumb movement. Users can
operate FingerSlider with either their hand side-on (Figure 2a)
or palm-up (Figure 2b) relative to the Leap Motion, with the
side-facing mode being primary.

Finger-to-attribute mapping was guided by ergonomic principles, cognitive load theory [29], and Norman’s intuitive
design principles [11] to reduce cognitive load and enhance
usability. While gestures can vary culturally [30], sliding is a
universally intuitive gesture common in touchscreen interactions, facilitating adaptability to mid-air interactions. Integrating sliders onto fingers leverages each finger’s characteristics
and their parallel structure for multiple simultaneous control
channels.
Finger-based sliders can promote spatial and muscle memory, enabling parameter control without constant visual hand
confirmation. The index finger, with its high flexibility and
precision [31], typically controls frequently used or highprecision parameters. The middle finger provides a stable
platform for parameters that require continuous, intuitive adjustment. The less independent ring and little fingers [32] can
manage essential but less frequently adjusted parameters.

IV. P RELIMINARY O BJECT M ANIPULATION S TUDY
To initially evaluate FingerSlider’s core concept for multiattribute object manipulation, we conducted a preliminary
controlled experiment. Each finger was mapped to a specific
control: the index finger to X-axis movement, the middle finger
to Y-axis movement, the ring finger to Z-axis movement, and
the little finger to object scale, with the thumb serving as the
interactor.
A. Methodology Overview
A within-subjects design was used with six participants (5
male, 1 female, aged 23-34) comparing FingerSlider to a baseline gesture system. The baseline system adopted a standard
VR gesture control paradigm widely used in current VR applications. Specifically, it utilized pinch gestures between the
index finger and thumb to manipulate virtual sliders displayed
in the user’s field of view. Users can perform a pinch-and-drag
motion to adjust slider values, with visual feedback provided
through on-screen UI elements. This method represents the
conventional indirect manipulation approach commonly found
in commercial VR systems such as Meta Quest’s default
interaction mode, supported by an additional visual interface
(Figure 5b). Both systems were implemented in Unity3D

FingerSlider demonstrated notable efficiency in simple,
single-parameter tasks (Task 1), averaging 24.5s compared to
the baseline’s 43.0s (Cohen’s d = -0.61, Table I). However,
as task complexity increased to involve multiple attributes

declined significantly, with completion times exceeding the
baseline (e.g., Task 4: 116.17s for FingerSlider vs. 57.0s for
baseline). This was primarily attributed to challenges in multifinger recognition and coordination, particularly involving the
ring and little fingers, leading to operational confusion and
unintended adjustments.
The System Usability Scale (SUS) score for FingerSlider
in this initial phase was 58.75 (Median: 57.50, SD: 4.37),
indicating marginal usability. Figure 6 highlighted that despite
performance issues in complex tasks, participants found FingerSlider’s core interaction model intuitive and preferred its
responsiveness for parameter switching. However, dissatisfaction arose from finger recognition inaccuracies, especially for
the ring and little fingers.
These preliminary results were pivotal. While FingerSlider
showed promise for intuitive single-parameter control, the
challenges with multi-finger coordination using all four fingers
were evident. The optical tracking system struggled to reliably
distinguish the movements of the ring and little fingers, especially when they occurred concurrently. This led to refining

V. S PECIFIC TASK E XPERIMENT
A. System Adjustment
Findings from the preliminary study indicated FingerSlider’s strength in single-parameter tasks (24.5s vs. 43.0s,
Cohen’s d = -0.61) and its fluid interaction, but multi-finger
operations with ring and little fingers suffered from recognition
issues. Consequently, we developed an optimized FingerSlider
version focusing on its core strengths by removing ring and
little finger functionality, concentrating control through the
index and middle fingers. To further enhance stability, we
enlarged the slider bar and central interaction button by 1 mm
in width and height (to 9 mm), allowing them to protrude
slightly above the ghost hand model. This aimed to facilitate
smoother thumb interaction and reduce recognition errors.
B. Comparison Study Design
To comprehensively evaluate FingerSlider’s usability in a
practical scenario, we designed a comparative experiment
focused on VR reading, a common task requiring precision
and comfort. Referencing established methodologies [27], we
aimed to verify enhancements in operational stability, user
experience, and usability. The baseline system featured an
identical reading interface to FingerSlider (Figure 7) for fair
comparison. This interface included a right vertical scroll bar
(controlled by index finger sliding in FingerSlider) and a
bottom horizontal scroll bar for page turning (controlled by
middle finger sliding in FingerSlider). The baseline system
used gesture-based indirect operations for these functions.

E. Experiment Measurement
We employed objective metrics (hand movement amplitude, task completion time) and subjective evaluations (questionnaires on user satisfaction, operational smoothness, system preference). Hand movement was measured via a custom real-time hand-tracking system (MediaPipe Hands in
Python/PyQt5) extracting 3D coordinates of 21 hand landmarks from webcam input.
F. Results
Statistical comparisons between FingerSlider and the baseline system were performed using paired t-tests with α = 0.05.
Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen’s d to quantify the
magnitude of differences. For SUS score comparisons between
the preliminary and main experiments, Welch’s t-test was
employed to account for unequal sample sizes.
1) Proposed System Demonstrates Greater Stability:
Heatmaps (Figures 8, 9) and scatter plots (Figures 10, 11)
revealed FingerSlider’s higher operational stability. Baseline
system heatmaps (e.g., Figures 9b, 8b) showed scattered hand
movements over a broader area, especially in long-text tasks.
This aligns with common observations of indirect interaction
and was corroborated by scatter plots showing high trajectory
dispersion. Baseline users often made large hand movements
and repeated adjustments. Feedback indicated the baseline’s
pinch gesture frequently led to unintended slider deselection.
In contrast, FingerSlider concentrated hand movements
in a smaller, well-defined area (Figures 9a, 8a), reflecting
more stable, controlled operations. Its scatter plots showed

baseline system required longer operation times. In the longtext reading task, the baseline averaged 3:07.48 (Figure 10).
FingerSlider showed clear efficiency advantages. Integrating
the slider onto the hand allowed real-time status monitoring while focusing on content, reducing attention shifts and
cognitive overhead (supporting H3). In the long-text task,
FingerSlider was over 30% faster (02:16.09 vs. 03:07.48).
Even in short-text tasks (01:39.10 vs. 01:43.18, Figure 11),

3) Proposed System Achieved High User Satisfaction:
Comparative evaluation (Figure 12) showed FingerSlider significantly outperformed the baseline in “Ease of use” (81.2%
vs. 18.8%) and “Overall experience satisfaction” (75.0% vs.
25.0%), achieving high user approval (supporting H2). For
“Smoother content Browse,” FingerSlider performed slightly,
but not significantly, lower; feedback suggested occasional
Leap Motion over-sensitivity caused unintended page jumps.
FingerSlider achieved an average System Usability Scale
(SUS) score of 82.81 (Median: 83.75, Table IV), indicating
high usability. This was a significant improvement from the
preliminary study’s 58.75 (a 40.95% increase, p < 0.001, Cohen’s d = 3.64, Table V), underscoring the two-finger design’s
effectiveness. The SUS score distribution (Figure 13) showed
most participants rated the system positively on learnability,
functionality, and intuitive operation.
Some participants expressed minor concerns regarding
“Confidence in use” citing the need to adapt to gesture

Current limitations include sensitivity and mapping challenges, such as the need for movements with extended content
due to fixed finger lengths; future iterations should refine this
mapping and explore user-configurable sensitivity options. The
system also exhibited some dependency on hand morphology.
While effective for VR reading, its application scope needs
broadening. Although the preliminary study (n = 6) was
small, the main study (n=16) demonstrated statistically significant improvements (e.g., SUS score increase, p < 0.001,

larger, more diverse participant groups. The two-finger system

Future work will focus on advanced gesture recognition
methods to enhance accuracy and accommodate wider hand
variations. We also aim to incorporate a broader range of
gestures (e.g., taps, fist gestures) and explore integration with
haptic feedback. Furthermore, we plan to investigate adaptive
algorithms for dynamic sensitivity adjustment and personalized control mappings.
VII. C ONCLUSION

G. Discussion
Post-experiment interviews revealed strong positive impressions. Participants found FingerSlider intuitive, smooth,
interactive, and comfortable for long-term use due to its
minimal body movement, which contrasts with the baseline’s
larger arm movements. One noted, “Using the FingerSlider
system doesn’t require additional body movement...much more
comfortable for long-term use”.
We observed participants subconsciously adapting to minor
misalignments between the slider and finger flexion. This
highlights users’ natural adaptive capabilities. The positive reception underscores that VR control granularity should match
the dexterity of the input mechanism. Mapping controls to
fine finger motor skills qualitatively improved comfort and
precision.
However, occasional mistouches occurred. While reduced,
optical gesture recognition was sometimes overly sensitive.
